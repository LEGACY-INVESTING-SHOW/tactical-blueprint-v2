Tax-free in, tax-free growth, tax-free out.

Slide #30
Let me explain why. When you form a single-member LLC, which is what most people create, the IRS classifies it as a "disregarded entity." That's their actual term for it. Disregarded. As in, they pretend it doesn't exist. All the income from that LLC flows straight through to your personal tax return. It goes on Schedule C. Gets taxed at your regular income tax rates. Gets hit with self-employment tax at 15.3% on top of that, which covers both the employer and employee portions of Social Security and Medicare. So not only did the LLC not save you money, if you're earning income through it without any strategy behind it, you might actually be paying more in taxes than you were as a W-2 employee. Because at least as a W-2, your employer covers half of the payroll taxes. As a self-employed person filing on Schedule C, you're paying the full 15.3% yourself. The LLC gave you one thing: liability protection. If someone sues your business, they can't come after your personal home or savings. So the LLC isn’t useless but it has nothing to do with taxes.

Slide #31
I use this analogy with every client I talk to. Your LLC is a container. Think of it like a box. The box itself has no value. It's just cardboard. What matters is what you put inside the box. The activity, the income, the documentation, the elections you make with the IRS. Forming an LLC without a business activity inside it is like buying an empty safe and expecting to find cash in it. The safe doesn't generate money. It just holds and protects what you put inside. People rush to form the LLC because it feels like they're doing something. It feels productive. It feels official. You've got your certificate, your EIN, your business bank account. But if there's no real business activity generating real income, the LLC is just an empty container sitting in a drawer.

Once your business is generating meaningful profit, you can elect to have your LLC taxed as an S-Corporation. Here's why that matters.

Slide #33
Let's say your side business generates $80,000 in net profit this year. Without an S-Corp election, that entire $80,000 goes on Schedule C. You pay income tax on it, plus 15.3% self-employment tax. On $80K, the self-employment tax alone is roughly $11,300. That's on top of your federal and state income taxes. Now, with an S-Corp election, you pay yourself a reasonable salary. Let's say $40,000. You pay payroll taxes on that $40,000. The remaining $40,000 is treated as a distribution. Distributions from an S-Corp are not subject to self-employment tax. So instead of paying self-employment tax on $80,000, you're paying it on $40,000. That's roughly $5,650 saved in payroll taxes alone. Every single year. But here's the catch. The IRS says your salary has to be "reasonable." You can't pay yourself $10,000 on an $80,000 business and call the rest a distribution. That's a red flag. The salary needs to reflect what someone in your role and industry would actually earn. And an S-Corp election doesn't make sense for everyone. If your business is making $20,000 a year, the accounting and payroll costs of running an S-Corp might eat up the savings. Generally, the S-Corp starts making sense once your net profit is consistently above $40,000 to $50,000. Below that, the complexity isn't worth it. The point is: the election matters. The timing matters. The salary matters. And none of it works without a real business generating real profit underneath it.
